Dark Patterns: How Swiggy, Amazon, and Netflix Play Psychological Tricks on You

Have you ever planned to order a ₹200 meal, but the bill magically inflated to ₹280? Or tried to cancel a subscription and given up? Welcome to dark patterns.

Dark patterns are deceptive UI designs intentionally crafted to trick users into taking actions the company wants, rather than what the user intends. This isn't a bug—it's deliberate psychological manipulation. Below, I expose common dark patterns used by major apps with practical examples and advice for readers in India.

1. Swiggy & Zomato: "Sneak into Basket" and Hidden Costs

Food delivery apps are some of the biggest culprits. Their most common trick is quietly adding extra charges on the final checkout page.

How it works: When you add food to your cart, the price looks reasonable. But on the payment screen, a "Packaging fee," "Handling fee," "Surge fee," and auto-selected "Donations" or "Tips" are suddenly tacked on.

This trick is known as Sneak into Basket. Pre-ticked boxes (₹2 donation, default ₹20 tip) inflate the final bill. People ordering food are often in a hurry and forget to uncheck hidden fees.

Visual example: A Swiggy checkout where the total jumps due to pre-checked donation boxes and vaguely defined "convenience fees."

2. Amazon: The "Roach Motel" (Easy to Check In, Hard to Leave)

Amazon makes purchasing effortless, but canceling subscriptions can be a maze.

How it works: Subscribing is often one click, but canceling Prime is buried. This is the Roach Motel—easy to enter, hard to exit.

During cancellation, you'll be repeatedly asked "Are you sure?" The button to stay subscribed is brightly styled, while the actual cancel action is dull or hidden. This is Misdirection, steering users toward keeping the subscription.

Visual example: Prime's cancellation flow with a bright "Keep My Benefits" option and a muted "Cancel" link.

3. Netflix: Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO) & Misdirection

Netflix looks clean, but subtly nudges users toward pricier plans.

How it works: When creating an account, Netflix often shows the most expensive plan selected by default. Cheaper plans may be hidden behind a small "See all plans" link.

This combination of Confirmshaming and Misdirection aims to get you to pick a higher tier without realizing cheaper options exist.

Visual example: Pricing screens favoring the premium plan while hiding the basic option behind a tiny link.

What Are the Rules in India?

The Indian government is taking action. The Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) has issued guidelines banning dark patterns in e-commerce.

Examples now considered illegal include:

  • False Urgency: Fake timers or "Only 2 items left" when inventory is ample.
  • Forced Action: Forcing users to sign up or buy unrelated products to use fundamental features.
  • Subscription Traps: Making cancellation intentionally complex or hiding the cancel option.

How Can You Protect Yourself?

  1. Slow Down: Before hitting "Pay", review the price breakdown and uncheck pre-ticked boxes.
  2. Read the Buttons: Don't trust colors — read the text. Designers use colors to mislead.
  3. Call Out Brands: Take screenshots and expose dark patterns on platforms like X or LinkedIn; public pressure forces change.
